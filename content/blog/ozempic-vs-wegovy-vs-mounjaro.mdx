## The GLP-1 Landscape: More Options Than Ever

If you've been researching weight loss medication, you've likely come across a dizzying array of brand names — Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, Zepbound, Rybelsus. They're all called "GLP-1 medications," but they're not all the same.

This guide cuts through the confusion. We'll compare the three most commonly prescribed GLP-1 options for weight loss: **Ozempic**, **Wegovy**, and **Mounjaro** — so you can have an informed conversation with your provider.

## Ozempic (Semaglutide)

**FDA-approved for:** Type 2 diabetes management

**Used off-label for:** Weight loss

Ozempic was originally developed to help people with Type 2 diabetes control blood sugar. It quickly became famous when patients — and their doctors — noticed significant weight loss as a side effect.

### How it works
Ozempic mimics GLP-1, slowing digestion, reducing appetite, and improving insulin sensitivity.

### Dosing
- Starting dose: 0.25 mg weekly for 4 weeks
- Maintenance: 0.5–2 mg weekly

### Who it's best for
- People with Type 2 diabetes who also want to lose weight
- Patients whose insurance covers it for diabetes (less costly route)
- Those who have tried other approaches without success

### The catch
Ozempic is not FDA-approved for weight loss specifically. Providers prescribe it off-label, which can affect insurance coverage and pharmacy availability.

## Wegovy (Semaglutide)

**FDA-approved for:** Chronic weight management in adults with obesity or overweight + health condition

Wegovy is essentially Ozempic's twin — same active ingredient (semaglutide), but at a higher dose and with FDA approval specifically for weight loss.

### How it works
Identical mechanism to Ozempic, but the higher maximum dose (2.4 mg vs. 2 mg) contributes to greater average weight loss.

### Dosing
- Starting dose: 0.25 mg weekly
- Escalates over 16 weeks to maintenance: 2.4 mg weekly

### Who it's best for
- Adults with BMI ≥ 30, or BMI ≥ 27 with a weight-related condition
- Patients who want an FDA-approved weight loss medication
- Those who tolerated lower-dose semaglutide well

### The catch
Supply shortages have been an issue since launch. Price without insurance can exceed $1,300/month, though manufacturer coupons and telehealth programs (like SlimRx) can significantly reduce cost.

## Mounjaro / Zepbound (Tirzepatide)

**FDA-approved for:** Type 2 diabetes (Mounjaro) and chronic weight management (Zepbound)

Tirzepatide is the newest class of GLP-1 medication — and arguably the most powerful. Unlike semaglutide, which only activates GLP-1 receptors, **tirzepatide activates two receptors**: GLP-1 *and* GIP (glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide).

This dual action is why tirzepatide consistently shows greater weight loss than semaglutide in head-to-head studies.

### How it works
By activating both GLP-1 and GIP receptors, tirzepatide delivers stronger appetite suppression, better blood sugar control, and greater fat burning than single-receptor agonists.

### Dosing
- Starting dose: 2.5 mg weekly for 4 weeks
- Escalates every 4 weeks to maintenance: 5–15 mg weekly

### Who it's best for
- Patients who need maximum weight loss results
- People with Type 2 diabetes seeking both glycemic and weight control
- Those who have not responded well to semaglutide

### The catch
Tirzepatide is newer, so long-term data (beyond 3 years) is more limited. Like Wegovy, it's expensive without insurance ($1,000–$1,200/month retail).

## Which One Should You Choose?

The honest answer: **your provider decides**, based on your health history, goals, and insurance situation. But here's a general framework:

**Choose Ozempic if:** You have Type 2 diabetes and your insurance covers it for that indication.

**Choose Wegovy if:** You want an FDA-approved weight-loss medication and can access it at a reasonable cost.

**Choose Mounjaro/Zepbound if:** You want the strongest available GLP-1 option and are willing to pay out of pocket or have good insurance coverage.

## What About Compounded Semaglutide?

Due to the FDA-declared shortage of Wegovy and Ozempic, many telehealth companies (including some in the SlimRx network) have been able to legally dispense **compounded semaglutide** — a pharmacy-made version that's significantly more affordable.

Compounded versions are not FDA-approved drugs, but they can be legally prescribed during a shortage period. Always ensure you're working with a licensed provider and an accredited 503B compounding pharmacy.
